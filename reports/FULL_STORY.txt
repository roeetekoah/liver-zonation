Scope: a 2-3 day graduate-hackathon re-analysis of the single-nucleus transcriptomic
component only — not the paper's imaging, immunostaining, protein, organoid, or spatial

## Scope and conclusion

This re-analysis examines the single-nucleus RNA-seq evidence from Gribben et al., Nature
2024, which reported that during human MASLD progression hepatocytes progressively lose
zonation and acquire cholangiocyte-like features. We focus only on the transcriptomic
single-nucleus component. We do not re-analyze the paper's imaging, immunostaining,
protein-level validation, organoid experiments, or spatial architecture.

Our conclusion is narrow but important:

In acquisition-matched MASLD needle-biopsy samples, hepatocyte transcriptional zonation is
preserved across the fibrosis axis. The apparent progressive loss of zonation in the
original single-nucleus analysis is largely driven by a sampling/source confound: the
healthy and end-stage groups are not acquired the same way as the disease-biopsy samples.
Restricted to comparable biopsy tissue and performed on raw, depth-controlled counts at the
donor level, we do not detect a large coordinated breakdown of hepatocyte zonation.

A separate genome-wide pseudobulk differential-expression analysis finds one main signal
across the biopsy disease axis: a small biliary/ductular marker burden in hepatocyte-
labeled nuclei, strongest at cirrhotic F4. Multiple diagnostics suggest this signal is
largely compositional — consistent with cholangiocyte ambient RNA and rare mixed/doublet
nuclei from the ductular reaction — rather than widespread hepatocyte transdifferentiation.
This source-attribution conclusion is not closed: a few genes survive ambient correction,
and a weak or rare hepatocyte-intrinsic inflammatory/plasticity state remains possible.

So the re-analysis does not overturn the paper. It challenges the single-nucleus
transcriptomic claim of progressive de-zonation across MASLD progression, and it reframes
the hepatocyte biliary-marker signal as a possible contamination/composition problem.

## Biological background: what "zonation" means

The liver lobule is spatially organized along the porto-central axis. Pericentral
hepatocytes (near the central vein) preferentially express one set of genes, including GLUL
and detoxification/CYP genes such as CYP3A4 and CYP2E1. Periportal hepatocytes (near the
portal tract) preferentially express another program, including ASS1, CPS1, PCK1, HAL and
ALDOB (urea cycle, gluconeogenesis).

"Zonation" means this division of labor is intact: different hepatocytes run different
programs by location. "De-zonation" means it breaks down — and it could break down in
several distinct ways: pericentral cells could disappear; zonal genes could turn off; cells
could start co-expressing both programs; the gradient could compress toward an intermediate
state; or the periportal-to-pericentral ratio could shift. Because these failure modes have
different count signatures, we did not rely on one global correlation score; we tested them
separately.

## The central confound: tissue source is tied to disease stage

The dataset is not one homogeneous disease series. The disease-biopsy samples and the
endpoint groups differ in how the tissue was obtained. The MASLD spectrum F0-F4 consists of
16-gauge needle biopsies. The healthy controls come from deceased-donor transplant-organ
tissue, and the end-stage samples from explanted cirrhotic organs. Organ tissue and explants
experience ischemia, procurement stress, handling delay, and dissociation effects that fresh
needle biopsies do not.

So the apparent axis healthy -> MASLD -> MASH -> cirrhosis -> end-stage is also partly an
acquisition axis: deceased-donor organ tissue -> needle biopsy -> explanted organ. This is
the load-bearing methodological issue: if disease stage is collinear with tissue source, a
comparison across the full series cannot separate disease biology from procurement and
sampling. The main disease-axis analysis therefore excludes the healthy and end-stage groups
and focuses on the acquisition-matched biopsy samples. The endpoint groups remain informative
descriptively, but they are not clean evidence for a progressive trajectory.

## Preliminary check: end-stage lobe is not the explanation

The end-stage explants were sampled from multiple lobes, including the caudate lobe, which
has unusual anatomy and can behave differently in cirrhosis. Within end-stage hepatocytes,
zonation-marker detection rates were broadly similar across right/caudate/left lobes — GLUL
~0.350/0.343/0.297, ALDOB 0.821/0.837/0.838, CPS1 0.826/0.874/0.879. This clears the narrow
"caudate tissue is just different" sub-confound, but it does not solve the larger problem
that explants and biopsies are fundamentally different acquisition contexts.

## Procurement stress is visible and source-collinear

If organ/explant samples carry procurement stress, it should show in stress-response genes.
We measured a stress module of immediate-early and heat-shock genes (FOS, JUN, JUNB, JUND,
ATF3, DUSP1, HSPA1A, HSPA1B). To avoid mistaking sequencing depth for biology, we used raw
UMI counts and down-thinned each nucleus to a common 1,500-UMI budget (nuclei below it
dropped), so a count difference is not just deeper sequencing.

The module showed a strong source pattern (mean stress UMIs per nucleus): biopsy ~0.074,
healthy deceased-donor ~0.282, end-stage explant ~1.675. The same pattern holds on an
ambient-robust metric (at least two stress UMIs per nucleus); end-stage stays ~20x higher
than biopsy, stable across downsampling budgets.

The key control is cross-lineage: the immediate-early stress increase in end-stage vs biopsy
is similar in hepatocytes and in endothelial cells (~18x in both). Endothelial cells are not
where hepatocyte zonation is tested, so this is not a hepatocyte-zonation-specific program —
it is an organ-wide acute handling/procurement signal. This supports excluding the healthy
and end-stage groups from the clean disease-axis analysis.

## Why raw counts and depth normalization

The core questions are count questions: is a marker detected, how many molecules are present,
does a nucleus truly co-detect two programs? Normalization methods such as SCT are useful for
clustering and visualization but distort absolute count interpretation, and are not ideal for
deciding one stray ambient molecule versus robust expression. So the main zonation tests use
raw counts after down-thinning every nucleus to the same total budget, and a marker counts as
"on" only with at least two UMIs. A single-UMI event can easily be ambient RNA; two or more is
not perfect proof but is more robust.

## The main zonation test: count-based anchor classification

For each hepatocyte nucleus in the biopsy samples we asked whether it expresses a pericentral
anchor program (GLUL, CYP3A4), a periportal anchor program (ASS1, PCK1, HAL, ALDOB), both, or
neither, classifying it as PC-anchor, PP-anchor, dual, or null. The unit of inference is the
donor, not the nucleus: we summarize each donor's hepatocyte fractions per class and compare
donor-level fractions across fibrosis stage. This directly tests the failure modes:

- Pericentral depletion would drop the PC-anchor fraction. It does not — broadly flat and
  non-monotone across biopsy stages (36/19/23/22/21% F0-F4).
- Co-expression would raise robust dual-positives. At a one-UMI threshold dual cells look
  common, but at the ambient-robust two-UMI threshold the dual fraction collapses to roughly
  0.2-0.6% and does not trend up — much of the apparent low-level co-expression is ambient RNA.
- Turn-off would raise the double-negative (null) fraction. No strong monotone increase.
- A composition shift would move the periportal-to-pericentral ratio. Broadly flat/non-monotone.
- Gradient compression would move cells off the poles into the middle of the axis. There is at
  most a mild, non-monotone middle-ward drift that peaks before F4 and partly reverts; the
  poles remain present, the gradient is not collapsed.

Together the biopsy-only count evidence supports preserved hepatocyte transcriptional
zonation, and it is robust across a sensitivity grid (alternate pericentral definitions,
thresholds, periportal requirements, ALDOB in/out, CPS1-based, strict identity) — the
conclusion does not rest on one favorable panel.

## Why an earlier relative-ruler approach was abandoned

An earlier analysis used a z-scored relative coordinate (standardize the two programs, form a
relative axis, read de-zonation off its spread/correlation). We abandoned it for two reasons.
First, it was source-confounded: including healthy and end-stage tissue created an apparent
collapse that largely disappeared on biopsy-only data. Second, it was mechanism-blind: a
smaller spread can come from true co-expression, complete turn-off, reduced depth, or noise —
biologically different scenarios the ruler cannot separate. The count-based anchor
classification was built to separate them. The lesson: a global anti-correlation or relative-
coordinate metric can be visually compelling but biologically ambiguous.

## End-stage explants are heterogeneous, not one clean program

Excluded from the clean axis but still descriptively informative: analyzed individually, the
five end-stage organs do not show one uniform zonation phenotype. One retains a strong
pericentral fraction; one shows a large dual/co-expression signal; others shift strongly
periportal, one to near-total pericentral collapse. PC-anchor fraction ranges ~3% to ~50%;
PP:PC ratio ranges ~0.13 to over 20. If they all moved together one might call them a coherent
terminal state despite the confound; instead they diverge. Pooling them into one average
correlation hides this and can manufacture the impression of a single "de-zonation" phenotype.
The safe reading: dramatic, heterogeneous disruption under a confounded acquisition context —
not clean evidence for a progressive biopsy-axis trajectory.

## Equivalence framing: bounding the absence of a large shift

A negative result is not automatically preservation, so we added an equivalence-style bound.
Across biopsy F1 to F4 the estimated change in pericentral-anchor fraction is small (about
+2.4 percentage points) with a wide interval (small F4 n). The data exclude a large
compartment-scale shift on the order of 20 percentage points; the better-populated F1-to-F3
interior bounds it to roughly 12 percentage points. They do NOT exclude subtle drift below
about 10 percentage points (donor variation + small F4 sample limit resolution). The correct
claim: large biopsy-axis de-zonation is bounded against; subtle drift is not ruled out.

## Genome-wide pseudobulk differential expression

To ask whether any other hepatocyte program changes across the matched biopsy axis, we ran
genome-wide DGE. For each donor, raw UMIs were summed across hepatocyte nuclei (one profile
per donor — avoiding treating nuclei from one person as independent replicates), then analyzed
with edgeR (TMM normalization, negative-binomial quasi-likelihood, robust dispersion). The
main contrast was cirrhotic F4 vs F1; the cohort had 38 donors across F0-F4, with F4 only four
donors, so F4-specific signals need caution.

The results play two roles.

First, they provide an INDEPENDENT COUNT-BASED CHECK on the zonation result. Zonation genes are
flat at donor-level expression: GLUL and the identity/detox set are not significantly changed
(GLUL FDR ~0.80), and housekeeping genes are stable. This is not the primary proof of preserved
zonation — the anchor classification is — but it is an independent count-based sanity check,
from a method (per-gene donor-level expression) that shares no machinery with the anchor
instruments, agreeing with it.

Second, the scan asks whether any other large hepatocyte program changes. Only a small set of
genes reaches FDR significance at F4-vs-F1, and they are not a broad metabolic, detox,
mitochondrial, cell-cycle, or stress program — they are mostly biliary/ductular/plasticity
markers (EPCAM, GRHL2, SPINT2, SOX4, SOX9, B3GNT3) plus CXCL10. The scan does not rediscover
widespread hepatocyte remodeling; it finds a biliary-marker burden inside hepatocyte-labeled
pseudobulk.

A gene-level scan cannot fully exclude weak coordinated pathway shifts (a pathway can move
modestly across many genes without single genes passing FDR). So the honest claim is not "no
other hepatocyte program exists." It is: no large single-gene hepatocyte program was detected
outside the biliary/ductular marker burden; formal gene-set testing is still needed to exclude
weak coordinated pathway shifts.

## Interpreting the biliary-marker burden

A biliary-marker burden in hepatocyte-labeled nuclei does not automatically mean
transdifferentiation. It could be genuine plasticity, ambient cholangiocyte RNA, doublets/mixed
nuclei, or misclassified rare states. Several observations favor a compositional explanation:

- The headline biliary genes are far more abundant in cholangiocytes than hepatocytes, so a
  little cholangiocyte leakage creates a large apparent hepatocyte signal.
- The cholangiocyte fraction rises at F4 (ductular reaction) — a richer ambient environment
  exactly where the hepatocyte biliary burden appears.
- True per-cell co-expression is rare (~0.4% of hepatocyte nuclei robustly co-express multiple
  biliary markers) — hard to reconcile with a broad transdifferentiation wave.
- Ambient correction with decontX cuts the significant hits from 64 to 34 and drops SOX4/SOX9
  below significance — consistent with ambient contribution.

But decontX is not a verdict: it can remove true hybrid biology if it treats unexpected lineage
markers as contamination, and it can also under-correct; and several genes (EPCAM, B3GNT3,
SPINT2, CXCL10) survive and need caution. CXCL10 should be read separately — an interferon/
inflammatory gene, not simply a cholangiocyte structural marker, and a candidate real
inflammatory hepatocyte response rather than contamination.

Proper conclusion: the biliary/plasticity signal is most consistent with a mixture of
cholangiocyte ambient RNA and rare mixed/doublet events associated with the ductular reaction,
but a rare or partial hepatocyte-intrinsic program is not excluded.

## Final interpretation

1. The full healthy-to-end-stage trajectory in the original single-nucleus analysis is
   confounded by tissue source; the three groups are not directly comparable as one clean axis.
2. Within acquisition-matched needle-biopsy MASLD, hepatocyte transcriptional zonation is
   preserved — multiple de-zonation mechanisms tested directly on raw, depth-normalized counts,
   none showing a large monotone breakdown, robust to marker definitions, corroborated by
   genome-wide pseudobulk expression.
3. The main genome-wide disease-associated signal inside hepatocyte-labeled nuclei is a
   biliary/ductular marker burden at F4 — overlapping the paper's biology, but here not
   sufficient evidence for widespread transdifferentiation; its pattern is strongly compatible
   with cholangiocyte ambient RNA and rare mixed/doublet events. CXCL10 remains a candidate
   real inflammatory signal.

Stated with boundaries: in biopsy-matched MASLD, single-nucleus hepatocyte expression does not
support progressive large-scale de-zonation. The strong zonation/plasticity signal is
concentrated in the confounded end-stage explants and in a small biliary-marker burden whose
source is not definitively hepatocyte-intrinsic. This corrects the transcriptomic
interpretation; it does not address the paper's imaging, protein, spatial, or organoid evidence.

## Remaining limitations and highest-priority next steps (future work)

- Pathway-level sensitivity (the biggest gap): a pre-specified gene-set test (CAMERA + ROAST on
  the same pseudobulk model) across hepatocyte functional pathways and cholangiocyte/ductular
  signatures, to exclude weak coordinated programs.
- F4 robustness: leave-one-F4-donor-out DGE and per-donor plots for the biliary genes, to show
  whether the signal is consistent across cirrhotic donors or driven by one outlier.
- Quantitative contamination accounting: estimate whether the observed cholangiocyte ambient/
  doublet burden is, in magnitude, sufficient to explain the apparent hepatocyte biliary counts.

Until then, the strongest defensible statement: zonation preservation across matched biopsy
tissue is well supported; the biliary/plasticity signal is real as a signal in hepatocyte-
labeled data, but its source is not resolved and is more consistent with compositional
contamination than with widespread hepatocyte transdifferentiation.
