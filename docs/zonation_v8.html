1   The question in one breath

A healthy liver is spatially organised: each liver cell sits at a position along a blood-flow gradient and does the metabolic job that suits its position. One recent paper measured that organisation in healthy human liver with the positions kept. Another profiled tens of thousands of liver cells across the full fatty-liver disease course, but with a method that throws the positions away. This project uses the healthy map to assign a pseudo-zonal identity score to each spatially-blind diseased cell, and then measures — rigorously — how that spatial organisation falls apart as the disease worsens, and whether the cells with the most degraded zonal identity are enriched for duct-like / biphenotypic (plasticity) programs. Crucially, "falls apart" is read from the population, not from any single cell's recovered coordinate (which we never treat as a true position): healthy zonation makes the two opposite gene programs mutually exclusive across cells, and de-zonation is when that exclusivity — not a label we assigned — breaks down.

Why it can score full marks It is not a reproduction of either paper — neither paper performs this cross-dataset decoding step. It runs on the exact machinery of this course (single-cell processing and clustering, differential expression, and Benjamini–Hochberg FDR). And it fits two days because every individual operation is standard; the originality is in how they are combined.

What we've already built (so the two days are execution, not setup)

All the slow, fiddly plumbing and de-risking is already done in preparation. Nothing below is the scientific result — those are produced live during the hackathon — but the scaffolding, the data conversions, and the method's positive control are finished and checked in. This is why two days is realistic.

2   The biology, from the ground up

2.1 What the liver does

The liver is the body's central metabolic factory: it buffers blood sugar (storing and releasing glucose), synthesises most plasma proteins such as albumin, makes the bile acids that digest fat, converts nitrogen waste into urea, and detoxifies drugs. The single fact that drives this entire project is that these jobs are not shared evenly among liver cells — they are divided up by where a cell sits in the tissue.

Two kinds of epithelial cell do that work, and the distinction between them is central to this project. Hepatocytes are the bulk "factory" cells — ~80% of the liver's mass — arranged in plates along the blood path; they do essentially all the metabolism, detoxification, plasma-protein synthesis and bile production. Their identity marker is albumin (ALB). Cholangiocytes are a small minority (a few percent) that line the bile ducts — the branching drainage plumbing (the "biliary tree") sitting at the portal corners — and they transport the bile hepatocytes make out toward the gut, without doing the bulk metabolism. Their markers are cytokeratins KRT7, KRT19 and SOX9.

Two things to hold onto. (1) These are different cell types, with normally fixed, non-overlapping identities — a hepatocyte expresses ALB and not KRT19; a cholangiocyte the reverse. Mature epithelial identity is supposed to be locked, so a cell expressing both ("biphenotypic") is a striking sign of identity coming unfastened — that is plasticity, and Paper 1's headline finding. (2) Keep this axis separate from zonation. Zonation is variation within the hepatocyte population (where on the porto–central axis a hepatocyte sits); the hepatocyte↔cholangiocyte distinction is a difference between cell types. They are two different axes — which is exactly why "de-zonation" (losing your position, still a hepatocyte) and "plasticity" (losing your cell-type identity) are distinct things our analysis measures with separate signatures (§2.5, §4.3).

What flows in and out. The liver has a dual blood supply, both entering at the portal triads: the portal vein (~75% of inflow) carries nutrient-rich, only moderately oxygenated blood drained from the gut, spleen and pancreas — so everything absorbed from digestion passes through the liver first; the hepatic artery (~25%) brings oxygen-rich blood from the aorta. The two mix in the sinusoids, flow past the hepatocytes to the central vein, and leave via the hepatic veins to the heart. So periportal ≈ "just in from the gut," pericentral ≈ "on the way back out to the body."

Bile is the greenish fluid hepatocytes secrete (bile acids/salts, bilirubin, cholesterol) that emulsifies dietary fat in the intestine and carries away waste. It drains the opposite way to blood: from tiny canaliculi between hepatocytes into the bile ducts of the portal triads, then the common bile duct → gallbladder (storage) and duodenum. Cholangiocytes are the cells that line those ducts.

2.2 The lobule and the blood-flow gradient

The liver's repeating unit is the lobule, a roughly hexagonal tile about 0.5–1 mm across. Blood enters at the corners through the portal triads and flows inward to a central vein at the centre. Along that journey — the porto–central axis — the blood gives up its oxygen and nutrients, so a cell's chemical environment depends entirely on how far along the axis it sits: relatively oxygen-rich at the portal edge (where the oxygenated hepatic artery feeds in), oxygen-poor at the centre.

2.3 Zonation: the gradient written into gene expression

Because the environment changes smoothly along the axis, hepatocytes specialise smoothly too. This division of labour is called liver zonation. Broadly, the periportal end runs much of the urea cycle (ASS1) and gluconeogenesis (PCK1, ALDOB), while the pericentral end runs glutamine synthesis (GLUL) and cytochrome-P450 xenobiotic metabolism (CYP2E1, CYP2C8). A key human-specific result of Paper 2, though, is that several functions long assumed periportal are actually pericentrally shifted in humans — the gluconeogenic gene PCK2 and urea-cycle enzymes ASL/OTC/NAGS among them — so we take the marker sets empirically from the data, not from the mouse textbook. Crucially the cell carries out this specialisation by switching genes on and off in proportion to its position. So a hepatocyte's set of active genes is, in effect, a readout of where it sits. That is the hinge of the whole project: if position is encoded in expression, then position can be decoded back out of expression — even after the physical location is gone.

2.4 The disease that degrades it: the MASLD spectrum

Both papers study MASLD (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease, formerly NAFLD), the commonest chronic liver disease, which advances through ordered stages:

Terminology: MASLD/MASH is the current name; the Paper 1 dataset and older literature label the same stages NAFLD/NASH. We use MASLD/MASH in prose but keep the dataset's own labels when reading its metadata.

Why it happens (in brief). MASLD is a disease of metabolic overload. Chronic excess calories and insulin resistance push more fatty acids into hepatocytes than they can burn or export, so triglyceride accumulates as droplets — steatosis. When the lipid load outstrips the cell's capacity it turns toxic (lipotoxicity): mitochondrial and ER stress, reactive-oxygen damage, hepatocyte injury and death. That damage recruits immune cells (inflammation = MASH) and activates hepatic stellate cells to lay down collagen scar (fibrosis → cirrhosis). Genetics (e.g. PNPLA3, TM6SF2) and the gut–metabolic milieu tune individual risk.

Where does de-zonation sit — mechanism or symptom? Mostly a readout: zonation is maintained by spatial signalling gradients (notably Wnt/β-catenin and oxygen), and metabolic stress plus disrupted Wnt degrade it — so de-zonation largely reports how far cellular identity has unravelled rather than causing the disease. But it is not cosmetic: the two ends run different jobs (pericentral drug-detox vs periportal urea/gluconeogenesis), so losing the gradient means specific liver functions fail in a specific order. Why we map it: (1) a quantitative severity/progression marker finer than histological staging; (2) it pinpoints which functions degrade first (e.g. pericentral CYP drug metabolism — directly relevant to drug dosing in liver disease); (3) it may flag early, pre-fibrotic damage; (4) it connects to the plasticity route linked to cancer risk. The payoff is staging, mechanism, and pointing therapy at which cells/functions to protect — not a cure by itself.

2.5 What breaks — and the need it creates

As disease advances, the orderly lobule degrades in two distinct ways, and keeping them apart is the whole point.

De-zonation is loss of normal zonal restriction within the hepatocyte population: genes that should be confined to one end of the porto–central axis become blurred, silenced, or co-expressed with the opposite program. (Co-expression of opposite ends is one important mode; global loss of a program and flattening of the gradient are others — §5.6 tells them apart.)

Epithelial plasticity is loss of identity across cell types: the boundary between hepatocyte and cholangiocyte (bile-duct) identity blurs, producing biphenotypic cells (bi = two, phenotype = identity) that express markers of both lineages. This is molecular, not physical — no cell crawls into a duct; the gene-expression programs mix. A healthy hepatocyte expresses hepatocyte markers (e.g. albumin, ALB); a cholangiocyte expresses duct markers (e.g. KRT7, KRT19); a biphenotypic cell shows both. That is striking because mature epithelial identities are normally fixed, so cells crossing that boundary signal identity coming unfastened — an abnormal disease/repair response, not clean regeneration. (Paper 1 reports such transdifferentiation with organoid + PI3K–AKT–mTOR evidence; the in-vivo directionality is complex, so we treat it as boundary-blurring, not a clean one-way hepatocyte→cholangiocyte switch.)

So the contrast is clean: de-zonation = a hepatocyte forgetting where on the axis it belongs; plasticity = a hepatocyte forgetting that it is a hepatocyte at all. They look like two depths of the same unravelling — which is why we measure the first quantitatively across the disease course and test whether it travels with the second.

De-zonation vs plasticity — they sound identical but aren't (and this is H3) Both are "a cell expressing two things it shouldn't at once," so they blur together easily. The difference is which two things, i.e. which boundary is being crossed:

Analogy: a factory worker who forgets which station they man (de-zonation) is still a factory worker; one who forgets they work in this factory at all (plasticity) is something else. H3 asks whether these are the same workers — i.e. do the hepatocytes that have most lost their position (high de-zonation) tend to be the same ones drifting toward bile-duct identity (high plasticity)? De-zonation is the milder, earlier slip; plasticity the deeper one — H3 tests whether losing your address predicts losing your job.
The need this sets up (read this before the next section) To measure de-zonation we would need two things we do not obviously possess together: (1) a trustworthy, quantitative map of healthy zonation — a ruler — and (2) a large population of diseased cells, spanning every stage, to lay against that ruler. The next section shows that exactly these two things exist — in two separate papers — and that nobody has put them together.

3   The data that exists — and the lucky gap

Our need has two halves — a healthy ruler and diseased cells — and there happen to be two papers, each supplying exactly one half. But to see why they fit together (and why neither alone is enough) we first need one idea about how the measurements are made.

3.1 Two ways to measure a tissue: keep the map, or dissolve it NEW

There are two families of technique, and the difference between them is our opportunity.

  • Spatial transcriptomics measures gene expression while preserving each measurement's (x,y) location in the tissue slice. The workhorse, 10x Visium, lays the slice on a slide printed with a grid of barcoded spots (~55 μm, a few cells each); every transcript inherits its spot's barcode, so position is retained. Higher-resolution variants — Visium HD (a fine 2–8 μm grid), MERFISH (imaging that counts a few hundred chosen genes molecule-by-molecule), and PhenoCycler (cyclic antibody imaging of many proteins) — trade breadth for resolution. Because position is kept, you can compute how any gene varies along the porto–central axis — i.e. build the zonation ruler.
  • Single-nucleus RNA-seq (snRNA-seq) dissolves the tissue first. Its flow: lyse the cells and release nuclei into a suspension; the nuclei, now floating in a tube, are pooled and shuffled; each is captured in its own droplet with a barcoded bead; reverse-transcribe, amplify, sequence. The catch is the suspension step: once nuclei are mixed in a tube, which lobule or axis-position each came from is gone forever. The pay-off is robustness — nuclei survive freezing and fibrosis that would destroy whole cells — which is the only reason late-stage, scarred disease tissue can be profiled at all.
Two different "losses" — keep them separate, they matter all the way through (1) Technical loss of coordinates: snRNA-seq dissolves the tissue, so we no longer know where a cell sat. This is a limitation of the instrument. (2) Biological de-zonation: in disease the cell itself loses its zonal identity. This is the phenomenon we study. The whole project is: use a spatial reference to undo loss (1) just well enough to measure loss (2).

3.2 Paper 2 — the healthy ruler

Yakubovsky, Bahar Halpern, Shir et al. (Itzkovitz lab). A spatial atlas of the healthy human liver from live donors. Nature (2026). doi:10.1038/s41586-026-10377-y.

This paper supplies our ruler. Its key move was to obtain genuinely healthy human liver from living donors (ordinary "normal" references come from brain-dead organ donors, whose livers are metabolically disturbed), and to profile 16 samples with the full spatial battery above plus snRNA-seq. From the spatial data it reconstructed a quantitative porto–central zone index per spot and identified the genes that vary most cleanly along the axis.

What to read & extract from Paper 2 Figure 2 is the one that matters — it is the zonation reconstruction. Panel 2a shows Visium slides coloured by the spot zone index from portal to central; panel 2b is the ranked set of zonated genes. The paper analyses 1,724 hepatocyte-specific genes, of which 1,141 show significant zonal differences (q<0.25), each with a gene-zonation score (Fig. 2b), and normalizes expression as z-scores of log10 UMI-sum-normalized values — match that normalization so the signatures transfer. Read the Methods paragraph on zonation reconstruction / "axis spot annotation" (they seed the axis using landmark genes) to understand how the zone index is defined. Concretely, pull from this paper: (i) the ranked zonated-gene table → your pericentral/periportal signatures; (ii) the landmark genes anchoring each end (pericentral CYP2E1, CYP2C8, GLUL, and the human-specific PCK2; periportal ASS1, ALDOB, PCK1); (iii) the location of its snRNA-seq data and the script that assigns zonation to those nuclei (your classifier training set, §6).

3.3 Paper 1 — the diseased cells

Gribben, Galanakis, Calderwood et al. (Vallier, Mohorianu, Allison labs). Acquisition of epithelial plasticity in human chronic liver disease. Nature (2024). doi:10.1038/s41586-024-07465-2.

This paper supplies the cells to test. It profiled 47 liver biopsies by snRNA-seq across the entire spectrum — healthy (n=4), MASLD/NAFLD (n=7), MASH (n=27), cirrhosis (n=4), end-stage (n=5) — yielding ~100,000 QC-passing nuclei including 69,426 hepatocytes. Its headline biology is transdifferentiation between hepatocytes and cholangiocytes, driven by PI3K–AKT–mTOR; for us, the relevant secondary observation is that hepatocytes lose zonation — but it reports this only qualitatively.

One paper has the map but no disease; the other has the disease but no map. Both are recent — Paper 1 (2024), Paper 2 (2026) — and each was built to answer its own question, so no one has yet used the healthy spatial ruler to decode the disease cohort. That untaken step is our project.

"But Paper 1 already showed zonation loss" — qualitative vs quantitative vs continuous Paper 1's zonation evidence (their Fig. 2) is a UMAP, a correlation of two marker genes, and microscopy images of a handful of marker proteins (GLUL, ASS1) in healthy vs end-stage tissue. That is qualitative (you look and see it's messier), discrete (two extremes: healthy vs end-stage), and narrow (a few markers). We add three things it lacks: (1) quantitative — a de-zonation number per stage with confidence intervals and a significance test, so we can say how much and whether it's real; (2) continuous — every one of the 69,426 hepatocytes gets a real-valued position on the portal↔central axis (not a portal/central bucket), and we track the metric across all five stages as a curve, not two snapshots; (3) transcriptome-wide — the position is read from the whole zonation program (the ~1,141 significantly zonated genes among 1,724 hepatocyte-specific genes) via an external healthy reference, not 2–3 markers by eye. In one line: they showed that zonation is lost; we measure how much, where on the axis, and with what significance, across the entire disease course.

4   The precise question

Research question Using the healthy porto–central zonation ruler from Paper 2, can we assign a quantitative pseudo-zonation coordinate to every spatially-blind hepatocyte in Paper 1, and so measure how, and how much, hepatocyte zonation degrades as MASLD advances from healthy to end-stage — and is that degradation linked to the transdifferentiation Paper 1 reported?
Why this matters — beyond "we made a map" De-zonation is not a curiosity; it is liver function failing in a readable order, because the two ends of the axis do different jobs. Concretely:
• Drug safety. The pericentral zone runs cytochrome-P450 drug metabolism (CYP2E1/CYP3A4). If pericentral identity blurs first (H2), drug clearance changes early in disease — directly relevant to dosing and drug toxicity in liver patients, a real clinical hazard.
• Ammonia / brain. The periportal zone runs the urea cycle (ASS1) that clears ammonia. Losing it drives hyperammonemia and hepatic encephalopathy. So which zone erodes when predicts which failure mode comes first.
• A finer severity readout. Histological staging (steatosis→MASH→cirrhosis) is coarse and subjective. A continuous, molecular de-zonation score could grade severity more finely and may flag early, pre-fibrotic damage before histology does.
• Cancer link. The de-zonation→plasticity axis (H3) connects to de-differentiation, a recognised route toward hepatocellular carcinoma — so the cells that lose identity are the ones worth watching.
• A reusable instrument. Once validated, the reference-decoder reads zonal identity out of the many existing single-nucleus disease atlases that have no spatial data — value far beyond this one dataset.
In one line: we are not labelling cells for its own sake — we are building a quantitative readout of how the liver's division of labour disintegrates, in an order that maps onto real clinical failure modes.

Each hypothesis below is what we hope to show (the alternative); every statistical test silently compares it against its null, the boring "no-effect" world, and a p-value measures evidence against that null.

4.1   H1 — does zonation erode as disease advances?

Claim: a single de-zonation score climbs steadily Healthy→NAFLD→NASH→cirrhosis→end-stage. The score is a population metric — e.g. the weakening of pericentral–periportal mutual exclusivity, or the narrowing of the coordinate's spread — not any single cell's position (see "Isn't this circular?", §5.4). Null (H0): the score is flat — unrelated to stage.

The test, plainly. Our stages have a natural order, so we ask whether the score moves monotonically along it, with two rank-based tools. Spearman correlation (ρ) measures whether two things rise or fall together by rank: ρ=+1 perfectly increasing, −1 perfectly decreasing, 0 no relationship — here, score vs stage-rank. The Jonckheere–Terpstra test is the dedicated significance test for an ordered trend across ordered groups; it returns one p-value for "the score increases step by step across the stages." (Spearman gives the strength; Jonckheere gives the formal yes/no.)

What counts as a pass: a significant trend (ρ>0, p<0.05) that survives donor-level resampling, so it isn't driven by a single patient. If it fails (still useful): a flat score means zonation is surprisingly robust (and would gently contradict Paper 1's by-eye impression); a rise-then-plateau means the damage saturates early.

4.2   H2 — which gene programs collapse, and where on the axis?

Claim: the collapse is not uniform — specific programs (e.g. pericentral CYP detox genes) lose their zonal pattern earlier/faster. Null (H0): a given gene's expression does not change across stages within its zone.

The test, plainly. We bin cells into pseudo-zones (portal / mid / central) and, within a zone, test each gene for change across stages — differential expression (DE). Testing ~20,000 genes at p<0.05 would give ~1,000 false hits from noise alone, so we apply Benjamini–Hochberg FDR, which turns p-values into q-values that cap the expected fraction of false positives among the genes we call significant.

What counts as a pass: a coherent set of genes at q<0.05 whose loss explains the collapse, ideally enriched for known zonated programs. If it fails (still useful): no significant genes means the collapse is diffuse rather than program-specific — itself a statement about how zonation breaks.

4.3   H3 — do the de-zonated cells become the plastic ones?

Claim: the hepatocytes that have lost zonal identity are the same ones drifting toward cholangiocyte identity (gaining KRT7/KRT19). Null (H0): de-zonation and plasticity are independent.

The test, plainly. We score each hepatocyte for a plasticity signature, split cells into "de-zonated" vs "still-zonated," and ask whether the de-zonated group has higher plasticity. The Mann–Whitney U test (a.k.a. Wilcoxon rank-sum) compares two groups by pooling and ranking all values and seeing whether one group sits at the top — no bell-curve assumption. Crucially we test this within stage / within donor (per-donor correlations, or a model plasticity ~ de-zonation + stage + donor) — not pooled across all cells, where late disease simply having more of both would fake a link.

What counts as a pass: de-zonated hepatocytes show significantly higher plasticity (p<0.05) and the two scores correlate positively. If it fails (still useful): independence would mean losing zonal identity and gaining duct identity are separate processes — also worth knowing.

Why the answer matters (the motivation — not "we filled a spatial gap") The point is not that spatial-by-stage is unoccupied. Three things make the answer worth having, none made redundant by a future real-spatial study: (1) a reusable reference-based decoder — once validated, it reads zonal identity out of the many existing dissociated disease atlases that have no spatial data; (2) a mechanistic ordering — which zonal programs lose legibility first (H2) predicts the order in which liver functions fail; (3) a principled failure analysis — when and how the healthy program stops being legible (silenced vs identity-mixed; Step 5b) quantifies how lobular identity disintegrates, and H3 ties that to the plasticity Paper 1 links to disease severity. In short: we characterise the disintegration of cell identity in chronic disease, quantitatively — not merely note that zonation drops.

Why this is not reproduction. Neither paper computed a cross-dataset zonation coordinate for the disease cohort, a stage-resolved collapse curve, or the de-zonation↔plasticity link. We use Paper 2 strictly as a decoder for Paper 1. And a negative result is still a result: if the collapse is non-monotonic, or driven by only some programs, or if the ruler itself dissolves at a stage, each is a real, defensible finding — because the method and its controls are the deliverable.

5   From data to a position-number: the computational logic

§2 promised that position is written into expression. This section turns that promise into a procedure, building it one forced step at a time: each tool appears only because the previous step created a problem that demands it. Nothing here is a tool for its own sake.

5.1 Where we begin: the data table COURSE

After sequencing, the data is one giant table: a row per gene, a column per cell, each entry the number of molecules (UMIs) of that gene caught in that cell. Tens of thousands of each; everything downstream is arithmetic on this table. Reaching it is the alignment of Exercise 1 (placing reads on the genome) and the molecule-counting of Exercise 2 (deduplicating PCR copies into unique counts) — but the published datasets hand us the finished table, so that is our starting line.

5.2 The table lies — so first we clean it (QC + normalization) COURSE

Two flaws make the raw table unsafe. First, not every column is a real cell. Empty droplets, debris, and dying or leaky nuclei produce junk columns; if we keep them they later masquerade as "confused" cells and fake the very signal we hunt. So we discard them — filtering on too-few molecules, too-few detected genes, or too-high mitochondrial content (a dying-cell tell). This filtering is quality control (QC). Second, the surviving numbers aren't comparable: a cell sequenced twice as deeply shows twice the counts for a purely technical reason. So we rescale every cell to a common depth, then take a logarithm — so that a doubling counts the same whether a gene goes 100→200 or 1,000→2,000, which is the multiplicative scale biology actually lives on (we use log(1+x), because log(0) is undefined and the table is full of zeros). This is normalization.

QC we inherit; normalization we redo — exactly why Paper 1's authors already cell-filtered their published object, so we inherit their QC (plus a one-line check that the kept cells are ALB-expressing hepatocytes); we do not re-filter from scratch. But we re-normalize from the raw counts, for three reasons. (1) A cross-dataset transfer needs identical preprocessing. We score Paper 1 against signatures derived from Paper 2, and apply a classifier trained on Paper 2 — a score only means the same thing on both sides if both datasets went through the same normalization, so we run one pipeline over both, starting from counts. Their object carries Paper 1's normalization; Paper 2 carries its own — mixing them is apples vs oranges. (2) Their processed matrix is gene-incomplete. A Seurat object's scaled-data slot usually holds only the ~2,000 highly-variable genes (covariates regressed out), so several zonation signature genes may be absent or differently scaled there; the raw counts slot has every gene — which is why our R script pulled counts, not their processed values. (3) Reproducibility. A transparent CP10k→log→z-score we control can be described and defended in the report, instead of inheriting an undocumented, software-version-specific pipeline (which the course expects us to understand, not hide).

5.3 The zeros are a trap — and the trap dictates the method COURSE

Most of the table is zeros, and a zero is ambiguous: the gene may be genuinely off, or on-but-missed. Only a fraction of a cell's molecules are ever captured, so a gene present in a few copies frequently reads zero by pure chance — the same Poisson sampling you met modelling library complexity in Exercise 2 (the probability of seeing none is e−λ, large when the expected count λ is small). The missed-but-present zero is called dropout, and snRNA-seq is especially prone to it because a nucleus holds little RNA. The conclusion is decisive and shapes everything after it: no single gene's value in a single cell can be trusted.

5.4 Therefore: read position from many genes at once (signature scoring) NEW

Because one gene is unreliable but the zonation program spans many genes (Paper 2 finds ~1,141 significantly zonated hepatocyte genes, §3.2), we read position from many genes pooled together. We don't need all of them: pooling even a few dozen well-chosen markers already averages out the random per-cell dropout. So a signature score is the (normalized, scaled, background-subtracted) mean expression of a curated gene set — the exact Paper 2 landmark set (20 genes) by default, or the larger ~100-gene expanded set when we want extra dropout-robustness. "Background-subtracted" means: from each cell's mean over the signature genes we subtract that same cell's mean over a matched set of random control genes, so the score reflects the signature specifically rather than how highly-expressing / deeply-sequenced the cell is overall (this is exactly what Scanpy's score_genes does). We build a pericentral and a periportal signature from Paper 2's ruler and define a single coordinate per cell:

zonation_coord = score(PERICENTRAL) − score(PERIPORTAL)

High = pericentral-like, low = periportal-like; a middle value is ambiguous — it can mean "expresses neither" or "expresses both," so a single cell's middling coordinate is only a candidate for de-zonation, confirmed by the population-level structural metrics below, not by the coordinate alone. The coordinate is a signed real number (sign = which end; magnitude = how strongly committed), centred near zero — not a 0–1 probability; probabilities come later, from the classifier (§5.5). (Aside on the supporting cast: PCA, UMAP and clustering COURSE are used only as scaffolding here — to isolate the hepatocytes and to draw a score-coloured map — because Paper 1 already provides cell-type labels. The result comes from the coordinate, not the picture.)

In code: src/steps/step4_score.py — coord = mean_z(pericentral) − mean_z(periportal), baseline gene sets in signatures/*_paper2_landmark.txt (= the exact Paper 2 set, the default; swap in _core / _expanded / _sensitivity for robustness).

Honesty guardrail The ruler is a 1D axis, so we recover a pseudo-zonation coordinate, never a physical (x,y) location. We say "reconstructed zonation coordinate," not "predicted spatial position."
What "de-zonation" means precisely — why it isn't circular, and what it can't see We define de-zonation operationally, as the degradation of three properties of the cell population — not of any single cell's coordinate, which we never treat as a true position:
• (1) Mutual exclusivity. In healthy liver a hepatocyte is high-pericentral or high-periportal, so the two scores are strongly anti-correlated across cells. De-zonation = cells expressing both at once → that anti-correlation weakens.
• (2) Spread. Healthy hepatocytes span the whole axis, so the coordinate is a wide distribution (typically a single broad hump — a continuum, not two peaks); collapse bunches cells toward the middle (narrow). We track variance/IQR — the width is the signal, regardless of the hump's exact shape.
• (3) Internal coherence. Do the pericentral genes still co-vary with each other — is the program still a coherent module? (Step 5b.)
Why this isn't circular: assigning each cell a coordinate from its expression does not predetermine any of (1)–(3) — a disease that preserved zonation would keep strong anti-correlation and wide spread, both fully allowed outcomes. The 1-D coordinate alone is ambiguous (a middle value = "expresses neither" or "expresses both"), which is exactly why the honest metrics are these structural ones.
What this can not see (honest scope): we measure loss of the expression program, not physical re-arrangement. If diseased cells kept their gene-identity but were shuffled in space (a periportal-type cell sitting where pericentral cells belong), our method — like any dissociated snRNA method — would report no change, because the positions were never recorded. Architectural scrambling with intact expression is out of scope; detecting it needs spatial data on the diseased tissue (the very thing that does not yet exist).
Reading the validation plot: why a single central peak is still zonation When we ran the ruler on Paper 2's healthy hepatocytes, the coordinate came out as one broad hump centred near zero — not two peaks — and that worried us at first. It shouldn't: a single wide hump is exactly what intact zonation looks like. Three things to hold together:
• Zonation is a gradient, not a switch. Cells sit at every position from portal to central, so the coordinate is a continuum. A histogram of a continuum is a single spread-out hump. You would only see two separate spikes if hepatocytes came in exactly two discrete states — which is not the biology. So unimodal ≠ "no zonation."
• The proof is in the slopes, not the histogram. In the same validation figure the pericentral marker CYP2E1 rises with the coordinate and the periportal marker ASS1 falls with it. That monotonic ordering is the evidence the axis is real zonation: the coordinate is sorting cells by genuine porto–central biology. If there were no zonation, those scatters would be flat clouds with no slope.
• De-zonation is therefore a change in shape, not a change in modality. We never expect "two peaks become one." We expect the one hump to get narrower across disease (variance shrinks toward the middle) and the CYP2E1/ASS1 slopes to flatten. That is what H1 measures (Fig. 4) — so the healthy single-peak-with-correct-slopes result is a pass, not a problem.

5.5 A stronger reader: a zone classifier, and why its confusion is the signal NEW

The signature score (§5.4) is robust but blunt — it gives one number and no sense of how sure we are. A classifier adds exactly that. Built up from scratch:

What a classifier is, here. We show a model many hepatocytes whose zone is already known ("training"), and it learns the mapping from a cell's expression to its zone. Then, for a brand-new cell, it outputs not a single label but a probability for each zone — e.g. [central 0.92, mid 0.06, portal 0.02].

Why the probabilities are the whole point. The shape of that probability vector measures confidence, and lost confidence is de-zonation. A healthy, clearly-zonated cell gives a peaked vector (the model is sure: 0.92 central). A de-zonated cell — now part-pericentral, part-periportal — gives a flat vector (0.4 / 0.35 / 0.25): the model genuinely can't tell, because the cell no longer commits to a zone. We compress "peaked vs flat" into one number, the entropy of the vector (low = confident, high = confused). The mean entropy per disease stage is our headline de-zonation meter, and watching each zone's confidence separately tests whether pericentral identity blurs before periportal (H2).

In code: src/steps/step4b_classifier.py — calibrated multinomial logistic regression trained on paper2_train.npz, held-out confusion matrix on Paper 2 first, then predict_proba on Paper 1 → per-cell entropy = −Σ p·log p.

The catch — and the trick that fixes it The catch. A classifier can "cheat" by latching onto technical quirks of whatever data it trained on instead of real biology. Paper 2's main data are Visium spots — small multi-cell tissue patches, measured by a different technology. If we trained on those spots and then asked the model about Paper 1's single nuclei, much of what it "learned" would be "Visium-vs-single-nucleus" technical difference, and its zone calls on Paper 1 would be untrustworthy.

The trick. Paper 2 also ran snRNA-seq — single nuclei, the same technology as Paper 1. So we train on Paper 2's snRNA-seq nuclei and apply to Paper 1's snRNA-seq nuclei: the same measurement type on both sides, so the model can't win by reading the technology — what carries over is genuine zonation.

But where do the training labels come from, if Paper 2's snRNA also lost its coordinates? Good question — and this is the crux. The labels do not come from the snRNA nuclei's own (lost) positions. Paper 2 profiled the same livers two ways: spatially (Visium, positions kept) and by snRNA-seq. It used the spatial data to define the porto–central zones, then transferred those zone labels onto its snRNA nuclei (mapping each nucleus to a zone via shared expression; their repo's parse_snRNAseq_combined_atlas.m). So the training set is snRNA expression (same platform as Paper 1) paired with a zone label borrowed from the spatial modality — labelled data after all. Our current fallback (until we port that mapping) labels each Paper 2 nucleus by the signature-tercile rule from §5.4; that makes the classifier a sanity baseline, and swapping in Paper 2's spatial-derived labels is the upgrade that makes it ground-truth.

A free sanity check. Scoring and the classifier are two independent routes to the same answer. On healthy cells, where zonation is intact, they should agree — and that agreement is built-in evidence the signal is real biology, not an artefact of either method.

Entropy is an interpretation, not a direct measurement A confused (high-entropy) prediction means "the model can't tell which zone" — but de-zonation is only one reason a cell might confuse the model. The others, in plain terms:
• Too little data in the cell. A poorly-sequenced nucleus (few molecules / few detected genes) simply doesn't give the model enough to decide — that's a technical shrug, not lost zonation.
• A cell unlike anything it was trained on ("out-of-distribution") — e.g. a stressed or dying cell. The model was only taught healthy zones, so anything weird comes out uncertain regardless of zonation.
• Patient/batch differences. Systematic technical differences between donors or sequencing runs can shift expression and muddy the calls.
• A mushy model. Some models report wishy-washy probabilities even when they should be sure ("poorly calibrated") — then high entropy is the model's fault, not the cell's.
• Loss of hepatocyte identity altogether (not just zonal identity) — a different, deeper phenomenon that also makes zone prediction hard.
So we only call high entropy "de-zonation" when it is not explained by those — i.e. it survives checking against the cell's QC metrics, its donor/batch, and the module-level diagnostics of §5.6 agreeing. Practically, use a calibrated classifier (multinomial logistic regression) so the entropy means what we think it does.

5.6 The honesty problem: when does the ruler stop being a ruler? NEW

Here is the project's deepest risk. The ruler is calibrated on healthy tissue, but disease may corrupt the very genes it relies on. A coordinate collapsing to the middle could mean (a) genuine de-zonation, (b) the marker genes globally crashing for unrelated reasons, so the tick-marks fall off the ruler, or (c) the signature dissolving into noise. The raw number can't tell these apart — so we measure the structure of the signature per stage: do the pericentral genes still co-vary with each other (is the ruler intact)? does the pericentral–periportal anti-correlation across cells weaken (true de-zonation, robust to global shifts)? do two random halves of the gene set give the same coordinate (reproducibility)? and is a module switched off (program lost) versus still on but co-expressed with its opposite (positional restriction lost)? These diagnostics are Step 5b, and they let us say which kind of breakdown we are seeing rather than assuming the convenient one.

5.7 Comparing stages without fooling ourselves (DE + FDR) COURSE

Once every hepatocyte has a coordinate, we bin cells into pseudo-zones and ask, within a zone, which genes change across disease stage — this is differential expression, and it produces the gene-level story behind the collapse (H2). The danger is volume: testing ~20,000 genes at p<0.05 yields ~1,000 false hits from noise alone. So we control the false discovery rate with Benjamini–Hochberg (Lecture 5): sort the p-values, find the largest rank k with p(k)≤(k/m)·q, and report q-values. Everything we call "significant" is at q<0.05, never a raw p-value.

In code: src/steps/step7_de.py — pseudobulk (donor×zone), per-gene interaction expr ~ coord + stage + coord:stage, multipletests(method='fdr_bh'); see the algorithm block in §6 Step 7.

The whole logic in one line table → clean it (QC, normalize) → trust no single gene (dropout) → pool genes into a coordinate (signature) → sharpen with a same-platform classifier (entropy = blur) → check the ruler still holds → compare stages with FDR-controlled tests.

Step 7 — Which genes drive it (zone-resolved DE, FDR-controlled)

Inputs: A4 + A2.

Bin hepatocytes into pseudo-zones (portal / mid / central terciles of the coordinate), then — to respect the donor as the unit of inference — pseudobulk: aggregate expression per donor × zone and test the stage effect on those profiles, BH-FDR across genes. A cell-level Wilcoxon/GLM looks powerful but is statistically invalid here (pseudoreplication).

Avoiding circularity (important). The pseudo-zones were built from expression, so we must not then claim the signature genes themselves as "drivers." Three guards: (i) exclude the signature/classifier genes from driver claims (or label them validation-only); (ii) use a held-out split — define the coordinate on one half of the zonation genes, test collapse on the other; (iii) best, fit per gene expression ~ coordinate + stage + coordinate×stage — the interaction term ("this gene's zonal slope weakens with stage") is the honest "lost zonal pattern" test, not a re-test of the genes that built the axis.

zone = tercile(coord)                              # portal / mid / central
for z in [portal, central]:
    PB = pseudobulk(expr, by=(donor, zone))        # donor x gene mean log1p-CP10k
    for g in genes_detected_in(z):                 # >=10% of zone's cells
        fit:  PB[g] ~ coord + stage + coord:stage  # OLS on the donor aggregates
        p[g] = pvalue(coord:stage)                 # does the ZONAL SLOPE change with stage?
    q = BH(p)
report q<0.05 EXCLUDING signature genes           # circularity guard (+ held-out split)

6.4   Optional bonus (stretch) — does the collapse track inherited MASLD risk? (Paper 3)

The question, sharpened. Paper 3 (Nat Genet 2026) maps MASLD inherited risk variants to the genes they regulate (snATAC-seq + a massively-parallel reporter assay → a list of risk-variant target genes). We ask one precise thing: are the genes whose zonal pattern collapses earliest (our Step-7 hits) over-represented among those inherited-risk target genes — more than chance? A "yes" means the cell-state breakdown we measure and germline susceptibility converge on the same molecular targets; a "no" means de-zonation is mostly an acquired, downstream process. Either answer is publishable-grade context, and neither is needed for H1–H3.

The exact test (this is the whole method). It is a gene-set overlap test — light, no new heavy data:

hits        = Step7 genes with q<0.05 AND |interaction effect| large   # collapse drivers
risk_genes  = Paper-3 risk-variant target genes (from its supplement)
background  = genes ACTUALLY TESTABLE in Step 7 (detected in the zone,
              survived pseudobulk) -- NOT all ~20k genes
# optionally expression-match the background to hits (same mean-expression bins)
k = |hits ∩ risk_genes| ; draw = |hits| ; success = |background ∩ risk_genes| ; N = |background|
p = hypergeom.sf(k-1, N, success, draw)          # scipy.stats.hypergeom  (or Fisher exact)
fold = (k/draw) / (success/N)                      # >1 = enrichment
report fold + 95% CI + BH-corrected p

Why the background matters (the easy way to fool yourself). Testing against all 20,000 genes guarantees fake enrichment, because both lists are biased toward highly-expressed liver genes. The honest background is the set of genes that could have been called in Step 7 (detected + tested), ideally expression-matched to the hit list. Interpretation: fold>1 with BH q<0.05 → genetics×cell-state convergence; fold≈1 → independent processes (de-zonation is acquired). Caveats to state: variant→gene mapping is imperfect; small hit lists give wide CIs; result is associational, not causal. Code: src/steps/step9_bonus_enrichment.py (~30 min, gene-list only). A second, even lighter stretch: Spearman-correlate the Step-6 collapse curve against a Paper 4 immune-infiltration trajectory across matched stages.

Why it's a strong bonus, not a dependency It is genuinely additive (germline genetics × somatic cell-state), cheap (one hypergeometric test you already know from Lecture 5's multiple-testing material), and strictly optional — the project stands fully on H1–H3 without it, so it can never sink the timeline.

6.7   Statistical rigor checklist

  • Significance always at BH-FDR q<0.05, never raw p.
  • Stage effect via an ordered trend test, not pairwise only.
  • Donor-level resampling, never 69k cells treated as independent.
  • DE is pseudobulk (donor × zone), with the H2 collapse tested via the coordinate×stage interaction on held-out genes — not a re-test of the genes that built the axis.
  • A positive control (Step 5) and a negative control (shuffled labels).
  • Classifier judged by held-out accuracy on Paper 2 before use on Paper 1.
  • Effect sizes reported beside p/q.
