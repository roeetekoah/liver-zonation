1   The question in one breath

A healthy liver is spatially organised: each liver cell sits at a position along a blood-flow gradient and does the metabolic job that suits its position. One recent paper measured that organisation in healthy human liver with the positions kept. Another profiled tens of thousands of liver cells across the full fatty-liver disease course, but with a method that throws the positions away. This project uses the healthy map to put a position back onto each spatially-blind diseased cell, and then measures — rigorously — how that spatial organisation falls apart as the disease worsens, and whether the cells that lose their position are the same ones that lose their identity entirely. Crucially, "falls apart" is read from the population, not from any single cell's recovered coordinate (which we never treat as a true position): healthy zonation makes the two opposite gene programs mutually exclusive across cells, and de-zonation is when that exclusivity — not a label we assigned — breaks down.

Why it can score full marks It is not a reproduction of either paper — it is a cross-dataset decode that neither team performed. It runs on the exact machinery of this course (single-cell processing and clustering, differential expression, and Benjamini–Hochberg FDR). And it fits two days because every individual operation is standard; the originality is in how they are combined.

2   The biology, from the ground up

2.1 What the liver does

The liver is the body's central metabolic factory: it buffers blood sugar (storing and releasing glucose), synthesises most plasma proteins such as albumin, makes the bile acids that digest fat, converts nitrogen waste into urea, and detoxifies drugs. The single fact that drives this entire project is that these jobs are not shared evenly among liver cells — they are divided up by where a cell sits in the tissue.

Two kinds of epithelial cell do that work, and the pairing matters later. Hepatocytes are the bulk "factory" cells (metabolism, detox, albumin, bile production). Cholangiocytes are a minority that line the bile ducts — the branching drainage plumbing (the "biliary tree") that carries the bile hepatocytes make out toward the gut. In a healthy liver these two identities are crisp and separate; hold onto that, because disease blurs the line between them (§2.5).

2.2 The lobule and the blood-flow gradient

The liver's repeating unit is the lobule, a roughly hexagonal tile about 0.5–1 mm across. Blood enters at the corners through the portal triads and flows inward to a central vein at the centre. Along that journey — the porto–central axis — the blood gives up its oxygen and nutrients, so a cell's chemical environment depends entirely on how far along the axis it sits: oxygen-rich at the portal edge, oxygen-poor at the centre.

2.3 Zonation: the gradient written into gene expression

Because the environment changes smoothly along the axis, hepatocytes specialise smoothly too. This division of labour is called liver zonation. The oxygen-hungry jobs — the urea cycle (markers ASS1, HAL) and gluconeogenesis (PCK2) — run at the periportal end; glutamine synthesis (GLUL) and cytochrome-P450 drug detox (CYP2E1, CYP2C8) run at the pericentral end. Crucially the cell carries out this specialisation by switching genes on and off in proportion to its position. So a hepatocyte's set of active genes is, in effect, a readout of where it sits. That is the hinge of the whole project: if position is encoded in expression, then position can be decoded back out of expression — even after the physical location is gone.

2.4 The disease that erases it: the MASLD spectrum

Both papers study MASLD (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease, formerly NAFLD), the commonest chronic liver disease, which advances through ordered stages:

HealthyNormal architecture and zonationbaseline
MASLD / steatosisFat droplets accumulate inside hepatocytessteatosis
MASHFat + inflammation + hepatocyte injury (ballooning)inflammation
CirrhosisScarring (fibrosis) replaces working tissuefibrosis
End-stageArchitecture destroyed; regenerative nodulesorgan failure

Why it happens (in brief). MASLD is a disease of metabolic overload. Chronic excess calories and insulin resistance push more fatty acids into hepatocytes than they can burn or export, so triglyceride accumulates as droplets — steatosis. When the lipid load outstrips the cell's capacity it turns toxic (lipotoxicity): mitochondrial and ER stress, reactive-oxygen damage, hepatocyte injury and death. That damage recruits immune cells (inflammation = MASH) and activates hepatic stellate cells to lay down collagen scar (fibrosis → cirrhosis). Genetics (e.g. PNPLA3, TM6SF2) and the gut–metabolic milieu tune individual risk.

Where does de-zonation sit — mechanism or symptom? Mostly a readout: zonation is maintained by spatial signalling gradients (notably Wnt/β-catenin and oxygen), and metabolic stress plus disrupted Wnt degrade it — so de-zonation largely reports how far cellular identity has unravelled rather than causing the disease. But it is not cosmetic: the two ends run different jobs (pericentral drug-detox vs periportal urea/gluconeogenesis), so losing the gradient means specific liver functions fail in a specific order. Why we map it: (1) a quantitative severity/progression marker finer than histological staging; (2) it pinpoints which functions degrade first (e.g. pericentral CYP drug metabolism — directly relevant to drug dosing in liver disease); (3) it may flag early, pre-fibrotic damage; (4) it connects to the plasticity route linked to cancer risk. The payoff is staging, mechanism, and pointing therapy at which cells/functions to protect — not a cure by itself.

2.5 What breaks — and the need it creates

As disease advances, the orderly lobule degrades in two distinct ways, and keeping them apart is the whole point.

De-zonation is loss of identity within the hepatocyte population: a hepatocyte forgets whether it is periportal or pericentral and starts co-expressing genes from opposite ends of the axis. The gradient blurs.

Epithelial plasticity is loss of identity across cell types: a hepatocyte starts drifting toward a cholangiocyte (bile-duct) identity. "Drift" here is molecular, not physical — the cell does not crawl into a duct; its gene-expression program shifts. A healthy hepatocyte expresses hepatocyte markers (e.g. albumin, ALB); a cholangiocyte expresses duct markers (e.g. KRT7, KRT19). In diseased liver — especially end-stage — one sees cells expressing both at once. Such a cell is biphenotypic (bi = two, phenotype = identity): molecularly part-hepatocyte, part-cholangiocyte. That is striking because mature epithelial types are normally fixed, so a hepatocyte turning duct-like signals that its identity has come unfastened — likely an abnormal disease/repair response, not clean regeneration.

So the contrast is clean: de-zonation = a hepatocyte forgetting where on the axis it belongs; plasticity = a hepatocyte forgetting that it is a hepatocyte at all. They look like two depths of the same unravelling — which is why we measure the first quantitatively across the disease course and test whether it travels with the second.

The need this sets up (read this before the next section) To measure de-zonation we would need two things we do not obviously possess together: (1) a trustworthy, quantitative map of healthy zonation — a ruler — and (2) a large population of diseased cells, spanning every stage, to lay against that ruler. The next section shows that exactly these two things exist — in two separate papers — and that nobody has put them together.

3   The data that exists — and the lucky gap

Our need has two halves — a healthy ruler and diseased cells — and there happen to be two papers, each supplying exactly one half. But to see why they fit together (and why neither alone is enough) we first need one idea about how the measurements are made.

3.1 Two ways to measure a tissue: keep the map, or dissolve it NEW

There are two families of technique, and the difference between them is our opportunity.

  • Spatial transcriptomics measures gene expression while preserving each measurement's (x,y) location in the tissue slice. The workhorse, 10x Visium, lays the slice on a slide printed with a grid of barcoded spots (~55 μm, a few cells each); every transcript inherits its spot's barcode, so position is retained. Higher-resolution variants — Visium HD (a fine 2–8 μm grid), MERFISH (imaging that counts a few hundred chosen genes molecule-by-molecule), and PhenoCycler (cyclic antibody imaging of many proteins) — trade breadth for resolution. Because position is kept, you can compute how any gene varies along the porto–central axis — i.e. build the zonation ruler.
  • Single-nucleus RNA-seq (snRNA-seq) dissolves the tissue first. Its flow: lyse the cells and release nuclei into a suspension; the nuclei, now floating in a tube, are pooled and shuffled; each is captured in its own droplet with a barcoded bead; reverse-transcribe, amplify, sequence. The catch is the suspension step: once nuclei are mixed in a tube, which lobule or axis-position each came from is gone forever. The pay-off is robustness — nuclei survive freezing and fibrosis that would destroy whole cells — which is the only reason late-stage, scarred disease tissue can be profiled at all.
Two different "losses" — keep them separate, they matter all the way through (1) Technical loss of coordinates: snRNA-seq dissolves the tissue, so we no longer know where a cell sat. This is a limitation of the instrument. (2) Biological de-zonation: in disease the cell itself loses its zonal identity. This is the phenomenon we study. The whole project is: use a spatial reference to undo loss (1) just well enough to measure loss (2).

3.2 Paper 2 — the healthy ruler

Yakubovsky, Bahar Halpern, Shir et al. (Itzkovitz lab). A spatial atlas of the healthy human liver from live donors. Nature (2026). doi:10.1038/s41586-026-10377-y.

This paper supplies our ruler. Its key move was to obtain genuinely healthy human liver from living donors (ordinary "normal" references come from brain-dead organ donors, whose livers are metabolically disturbed), and to profile 16 samples with the full spatial battery above plus snRNA-seq. From the spatial data it reconstructed a quantitative porto–central zone index per spot and identified the genes that vary most cleanly along the axis.

3.3 Paper 1 — the diseased cells

Gribben, Galanakis, Calderwood et al. (Vallier, Mohorianu, Allison labs). Acquisition of epithelial plasticity in human chronic liver disease. Nature (2024). doi:10.1038/s41586-024-07465-2.

This paper supplies the cells to test. It profiled 47 liver biopsies by snRNA-seq across the entire spectrum — healthy (n=4), MASLD/NAFLD (n=7), MASH (n=27), cirrhosis (n=4), end-stage (n=5) — yielding ~100,000 QC-passing nuclei including 69,426 hepatocytes. One paper has the map but no disease; the other has the disease but no map. Both are recent — Paper 1 (2024), Paper 2 (2026) — and each was built to answer its own question, so no one has yet used the healthy spatial ruler to decode the disease cohort. That untaken step is our project.

"But Paper 1 already showed zonation loss" — qualitative vs quantitative vs continuous Paper 1's zonation evidence (their Fig. 2) is a UMAP, a correlation of two marker genes, and microscopy images of a handful of marker proteins (GLUL, ASS1) in healthy vs end-stage tissue. That is qualitative (you look and see it's messier), discrete (two extremes: healthy vs end-stage), and narrow (a few markers). We add three things it lacks: (1) quantitative — a de-zonation number per stage with confidence intervals and a significance test, so we can say how much and whether it's real; (2) continuous — every one of the 69,426 hepatocytes gets a real-valued position on the portal↔central axis (not a portal/central bucket), and we track the metric across all five stages as a curve, not two snapshots; (3) transcriptome-wide — the position is read from the whole 1,724-gene zonation program via an external healthy reference, not 2–3 markers by eye. In one line: they showed that zonation is lost; we measure how much, where on the axis, and with what significance, across the entire disease course.

4   The precise question

Research question Using the healthy porto–central zonation ruler from Paper 2, can we assign a quantitative pseudo-zonation coordinate to every spatially-blind hepatocyte in Paper 1, and so measure how, and how much, hepatocyte zonation degrades as MASLD advances from healthy to end-stage — and is that degradation linked to the transdifferentiation Paper 1 reported?

Each hypothesis below is what we hope to show (the alternative); every statistical test silently compares it against its null, the boring "no-effect" world, and a p-value measures evidence against that null.

Hypothesis (alternative)Null (H0) we test againstTest
H1 — monotonic erosion. A de-zonation score rises Healthy→MASLD→MASH→cirrhosis→end-stage.The score is flat across stages — no association with disease.Ordered trend (Jonckheere–Terpstra / Spearman on stage rank)
H2 — zone-specific vulnerability. Particular programs (e.g. pericentral CYP detox) collapse earlier/faster.A gene's expression does not change across stages within its zone.Per-gene DE + BH-FDR
H3 — plasticity link. The most de-zonated hepatocytes are the ones gaining cholangiocyte identity.De-zonation and plasticity scores are independent.Correlation / Mann–Whitney

4.1   H1 — does zonation erode as disease advances?

Claim: a single de-zonation score climbs steadily Healthy→NAFLD→NASH→cirrhosis→end-stage. The score is a population metric — e.g. the weakening of pericentral–periportal mutual exclusivity, or the narrowing of the coordinate's spread — not any single cell's position (see "Isn't this circular?", §5.4). Null (H0): the score is flat — unrelated to stage.

The test, plainly. Our stages have a natural order, so we ask whether the score moves monotonically along it, with two rank-based tools. Spearman correlation (ρ) measures whether two things rise or fall together by rank: ρ=+1 perfectly increasing, −1 perfectly decreasing, 0 no relationship — here, score vs stage-rank. The Jonckheere–Terpstra test is the dedicated significance test for an ordered trend across ordered groups; it returns one p-value for "the score increases step by step across the stages." (Spearman gives the strength; Jonckheere gives the formal yes/no.)

What counts as a pass: a significant trend (ρ>0, p<0.05) that survives donor-level resampling, so it isn't driven by a single patient. If it fails (still useful): a flat score means zonation is surprisingly robust (and would gently contradict Paper 1's by-eye impression); a rise-then-plateau means the damage saturates early.

4.2   H2 — which gene programs collapse, and where on the axis?

Claim: the collapse is not uniform — specific programs (e.g. pericentral CYP detox genes) lose their zonal pattern earlier/faster. Null (H0): a given gene's expression does not change across stages within its zone.

The test, plainly. We bin cells into pseudo-zones (portal / mid / central) and, within a zone, test each gene for change across stages — differential expression (DE). Testing ~20,000 genes at p<0.05 would give ~1,000 false hits from noise alone, so we apply Benjamini–Hochberg FDR, which turns p-values into q-values that cap the expected fraction of false positives among the genes we call significant.

What counts as a pass: a coherent set of genes at q<0.05 whose loss explains the collapse, ideally enriched for known zonated programs. If it fails (still useful): no significant genes means the collapse is diffuse rather than program-specific — itself a statement about how zonation breaks.

4.3   H3 — do the de-zonated cells become the plastic ones?

Claim: the hepatocytes that have lost zonal identity are the same ones drifting toward cholangiocyte identity (gaining KRT7/KRT19). Null (H0): de-zonation and plasticity are independent.

The test, plainly. We score each hepatocyte for a plasticity signature, split cells into "de-zonated" vs "still-zonated," and ask whether the de-zonated group has higher plasticity. The Mann–Whitney U test (a.k.a. Wilcoxon rank-sum) compares two groups by pooling and ranking all values and seeing whether one group sits at the top — no bell-curve assumption. We also report a plain correlation between the de-zonation and plasticity scores across all cells.

What counts as a pass: de-zonated hepatocytes show significantly higher plasticity (p<0.05) and the two scores correlate positively. If it fails (still useful): independence would mean losing zonal identity and gaining duct identity are separate processes — also worth knowing.

Why the answer matters (the motivation — not "we filled a spatial gap") The point is not that spatial-by-stage is unoccupied. Three things make the answer worth having, none made redundant by a future real-spatial study: (1) a reusable reference-based decoder — once validated, it reads zonal identity out of the many existing dissociated disease atlases that have no spatial data; (2) a mechanistic ordering — which zonal programs lose legibility first (H2) predicts the order in which liver functions fail; (3) a principled failure analysis — when and how the healthy program stops being legible (silenced vs identity-mixed; Step 5b) quantifies how lobular identity disintegrates, and H3 ties that to the plasticity Paper 1 links to disease severity. In short: we characterise the disintegration of cell identity in chronic disease, quantitatively — not merely note that zonation drops.

Why this is not reproduction. Neither paper computed a cross-dataset zonation coordinate for the disease cohort, a stage-resolved collapse curve, or the de-zonation↔plasticity link. We use Paper 2 strictly as a decoder for Paper 1. And a negative result is still a result: if the collapse is non-monotonic, or driven by only some programs, or if the ruler itself dissolves at a stage, each is a real, defensible finding — because the method and its controls are the deliverable.

5   From data to a position-number: the computational logic

§2 promised that position is written into expression. This section turns that promise into a procedure, building it one forced step at a time: each tool appears only because the previous step created a problem that demands it. Nothing here is a tool for its own sake.

5.1 Where we begin: the data table COURSE

After sequencing, the data is one giant table: a row per gene, a column per cell, each entry the number of molecules (UMIs) of that gene caught in that cell. Tens of thousands of each; everything downstream is arithmetic on this table. Reaching it is the alignment of Exercise 1 (placing reads on the genome) and the molecule-counting of Exercise 2 (deduplicating PCR copies into unique counts) — but the published datasets hand us the finished table, so that is our starting line.

5.2 The table lies — so first we clean it (QC + normalization) COURSE

Two flaws make the raw table unsafe. First, not every column is a real cell. Empty droplets, debris, and dying or leaky nuclei produce junk columns; if we keep them they later masquerade as "confused" cells and fake the very signal we hunt. So we discard them — filtering on too-few molecules, too-few detected genes, or too-high mitochondrial content (a dying-cell tell). This filtering is quality control (QC). Second, the surviving numbers aren't comparable: a cell sequenced twice as deeply shows twice the counts for a purely technical reason. So we rescale every cell to a common depth, then take a logarithm — so that a doubling counts the same whether a gene goes 100→200 or 1,000→2,000, which is the multiplicative scale biology actually lives on (we use log(1+x), because log(0) is undefined and the table is full of zeros). This is normalization.

QC we inherit; normalization we redo — exactly why Paper 1's authors already cell-filtered their published object, so we inherit their QC (plus a one-line check that the kept cells are ALB-expressing hepatocytes); we do not re-filter from scratch. But we re-normalize from the raw counts, for three reasons. (1) A cross-dataset transfer needs identical preprocessing. We score Paper 1 against signatures derived from Paper 2, and apply a classifier trained on Paper 2 — a score only means the same thing on both sides if both datasets went through the same normalization, so we run one pipeline over both, starting from counts. Their object carries Paper 1's normalization; Paper 2 carries its own — mixing them is apples vs oranges. (2) Their processed matrix is gene-incomplete. A Seurat object's scaled-data slot usually holds only the ~2,000 highly-variable genes (covariates regressed out), so several zonation signature genes may be absent or differently scaled there; the raw counts slot has every gene — which is why our R script pulled counts, not their processed values. (3) Reproducibility. A transparent CP10k→log→z-score we control can be described and defended in the report, instead of inheriting an undocumented, software-version-specific pipeline (which the course expects us to understand, not hide).

5.3 The zeros are a trap — and the trap dictates the method COURSE

Most of the table is zeros, and a zero is ambiguous: the gene may be genuinely off, or on-but-missed. Only a fraction of a cell's molecules are ever captured, so a gene present in a few copies frequently reads zero by pure chance — the same Poisson sampling you met modelling library complexity in Exercise 2 (the probability of seeing none is e−λ, large when the expected count λ is small). The missed-but-present zero is called dropout, and snRNA-seq is especially prone to it because a nucleus holds little RNA. The conclusion is decisive and shapes everything after it: no single gene's value in a single cell can be trusted.

5.4 Therefore: read position from many genes at once (signature scoring) NEW

Because one gene is unreliable but the zonation signal is spread across hundreds of genes (§2.3), we read position from many genes pooled together. A signature score is the average (normalized, scaled, background-subtracted) expression of a curated gene set; pooling averages out the random dropout so the per-cell number is stable. We build a pericentral signature and a periportal signature from Paper 2's ruler and define a single coordinate per cell:

zonation_coord = score(PERICENTRAL) − score(PERIPORTAL)

High = pericentral-like, low = periportal-like, middle/ambiguous = de-zonated. (Aside on the supporting cast: PCA, UMAP and clustering COURSE are used only as scaffolding here — to isolate the hepatocytes and to draw a score-coloured map — because Paper 1 already provides cell-type labels. The result comes from the coordinate, not the picture.)

Honesty guardrail The ruler is a 1D axis, so we recover a pseudo-zonation coordinate, never a physical (x,y) location. We say "reconstructed zonation coordinate," not "predicted spatial position."
What "de-zonation" means precisely — why it isn't circular, and what it can't see We define de-zonation operationally, as the degradation of three properties of the cell population — not of any single cell's coordinate, which we never treat as a true position:
• (1) Mutual exclusivity. In healthy liver a hepatocyte is high-pericentral or high-periportal, so the two scores are strongly anti-correlated across cells. De-zonation = cells expressing both at once → that anti-correlation weakens.
• (2) Spread. Healthy hepatocytes span the whole axis, so the coordinate is wide (and may show two humps — bimodal — if sampled cells cluster at the ends); collapse bunches them toward the middle (narrow). We track variance/IQR, robust whether or not the healthy shape is strictly bimodal.
• (3) Internal coherence. Do the pericentral genes still co-vary with each other — is the program still a coherent module? (Step 5b.)
Why this isn't circular: assigning each cell a coordinate from its expression does not predetermine any of (1)–(3) — a disease that preserved zonation would keep strong anti-correlation and wide spread, both fully allowed outcomes. The 1-D coordinate alone is ambiguous (a middle value = "expresses neither" or "expresses both"), which is exactly why the honest metrics are these structural ones.
What this can not see (honest scope): we measure loss of the expression program, not physical re-arrangement. If diseased cells kept their gene-identity but were shuffled in space (a periportal-type cell sitting where pericentral cells belong), our method — like any dissociated snRNA method — would report no change, because the positions were never recorded. Architectural scrambling with intact expression is out of scope; detecting it needs spatial data on the diseased tissue (the very thing that does not yet exist).

5.5 A stronger reader: a zone classifier, and why its confusion is the signal NEW

The signature score (§5.4) is robust but blunt — it gives one number and no sense of how sure we are. A classifier adds exactly that. Built up from scratch:

What a classifier is, here. We show a model many hepatocytes whose zone is already known ("training"), and it learns the mapping from a cell's expression to its zone. Then, for a brand-new cell, it outputs not a single label but a probability for each zone — e.g. [central 0.92, mid 0.06, portal 0.02].

Why the probabilities are the whole point. The shape of that probability vector measures confidence, and lost confidence is de-zonation. A healthy, clearly-zonated cell gives a peaked vector (the model is sure: 0.92 central). A de-zonated cell — now part-pericentral, part-periportal — gives a flat vector (0.4 / 0.35 / 0.25): the model genuinely can't tell, because the cell no longer commits to a zone. We compress "peaked vs flat" into one number, the entropy of the vector (low = confident, high = confused). The mean entropy per disease stage is our headline de-zonation meter, and watching each zone's confidence separately tests whether pericentral identity blurs before periportal (H2).

The catch — and the trick that fixes it The catch. A classifier can "cheat" by latching onto technical quirks of whatever data it trained on instead of real biology. Paper 2's main data are Visium spots — small multi-cell tissue patches, measured by a different technology. If we trained on those spots and then asked the model about Paper 1's single nuclei, much of what it "learned" would be "Visium-vs-single-nucleus" technical difference, and its zone calls on Paper 1 would be untrustworthy.

The trick. Paper 2 also ran snRNA-seq — single nuclei, the same technology as Paper 1. So we train on Paper 2's snRNA-seq nuclei and apply to Paper 1's snRNA-seq nuclei: the same measurement type on both sides, so the model can't win by reading the technology — what carries over is genuine zonation.

The one missing piece is a zone label for each Paper 2 training nucleus. Paper 2's spatial data define the zones, and its repository carries those zone assignments onto its snRNA nuclei (Step 1). A quick fallback: label Paper 2's nuclei by the same signature-tercile rule from §5.4.

A free sanity check. Scoring and the classifier are two independent routes to the same answer. On healthy cells, where zonation is intact, they should agree — and that agreement is built-in evidence the signal is real biology, not an artefact of either method.

5.6 The honesty problem: when does the ruler stop being a ruler? NEW

Here is the project's deepest risk. The ruler is calibrated on healthy tissue, but disease may corrupt the very genes it relies on. A coordinate collapsing to the middle could mean (a) genuine de-zonation, (b) the marker genes globally crashing for unrelated reasons, so the tick-marks fall off the ruler, or (c) the signature dissolving into noise. The raw number can't tell these apart — so we measure the structure of the signature per stage: do the pericentral genes still co-vary with each other (is the ruler intact)? does the pericentral–periportal anti-correlation across cells weaken (true de-zonation, robust to global shifts)? do two random halves of the gene set give the same coordinate (reproducibility)? and is a module switched off (program lost) versus still on but co-expressed with its opposite (positional restriction lost)? These diagnostics are Step 5b, and they let us say which kind of breakdown we are seeing rather than assuming the convenient one.

5.7 Comparing stages without fooling ourselves (DE + FDR) COURSE

Once every hepatocyte has a coordinate, we bin cells into pseudo-zones and ask, within a zone, which genes change across disease stage — this is differential expression, and it produces the gene-level story behind the collapse (H2). The danger is volume: testing ~20,000 genes at p<0.05 yields ~1,000 false hits from noise alone. So we control the false discovery rate with Benjamini–Hochberg (Lecture 5): sort the p-values, find the largest rank k with p(k)≤(k/m)·q, and report q-values. Everything we call "significant" is at q<0.05, never a raw p-value.

The whole logic in one line table → clean it (QC, normalize) → trust no single gene (dropout) → pool genes into a coordinate (signature) → sharpen with a same-platform classifier (entropy = blur) → check the ruler still holds → compare stages with FDR-controlled tests.

6.3 The steps

Step 1 — Build the ruler from Paper 2

Inputs: Paper 2 zonated-gene table (supplement) and/or zon_struct_all_full.mat; for 1c also combined_scRNAseq_atlas_M5M6M7M8.mat + parse_snRNAseq_combined_atlas.m.

1a — signatures. Load the zonated-gene table; keep genes passing the paper's own zonation q-value; rank by signed zonation score. Take the top ~50–100 pericentral and top ~50–100 periportal genes; intersect with genes present in Paper 1 (record how many survive). Freeze as pericentral.txt / periportal.txt.
1b — sanity-plot. Plot the zonation profile of the top ~10 genes each end to confirm monotonic, opposite gradients (catches sign/mapping errors early).
1c — classifier training set. Run / port parse_snRNAseq_combined_atlas.m to attach a zone label (or zone index binned into 3–5 classes) to each Paper 2 nucleus; export an (expression, zone_label) table.

Decision: if porting the MATLAB is slow, fall back to signatures only (1a–1b) for Day 1 and add the classifier later.
Artifact A1: signature lists + zonation-profile figure + zone-labelled training table.

Step 2 — Load & QC the Paper 1 hepatocytes

Input: GSE202379_SeuratObject_AllCells.rds.

2a — subset. Read the object; pull cell_type and stage from metadata; keep cells labelled Hepatocyte; confirm they express ALB and form a coherent cluster (quick PCA/UMAP).
2b — QC filters. Remove nuclei with very low UMIs or genes, or high mitochondrial fraction, matching the paper's stress-marker exclusion; optionally flag doublets. Record counts removed.
2c — normalize. Library-size normalize → log1p → per-gene z-score (§5.2), matching Paper 2's units so signatures transfer.
2d — bookkeeping. Build a per-stage, per-donor cell-count table (you will need donor IDs for the bootstrap in Step 6).

Artifact A2: clean normalized hepatocyte matrix (~69k) + QC plots + per-stage/per-donor counts.

Step 3 — Harmonize genes & fix the batch axis

Inputs: A1 + A2.

3a — ID mapping. Reconcile gene identifiers (symbol vs Ensembl, version drift) between the two datasets; report the mapping rate.
3b — intersect. Restrict signatures and classifier features to the shared gene set; report surviving signature sizes (if a signature shrinks badly, widen the top-N in Step 1).
3c — batch-robust features. Use z-scored or rank-based features so the score measures portal-vs-central, not snRNA-vs-Visium (§5.6).

Artifact A3: harmonization report + final gene sets.

Step 4 — Place every cell (score + classifier)

Inputs: A2 + A3 (+ A1 training set for 4b).

4a — signature scoring (baseline). Compute pericentral and periportal scores (Scanpy score_genes / our own background-subtracted mean); define zonation_coord = pericentral − periportal.
4b — classifier (headline). Train on Paper 2's zone-labelled nuclei (logistic regression or random forest), evaluate by held-out accuracy / confusion matrix on Paper 2 first, then apply to Paper 1; record predicted zone, full zone-probability vector, and entropy per cell.
4c — agreement. Check the scoring coordinate and the classifier's expected-zone agree on cells (especially healthy).

Artifact A4: per-cell table [cell_id, donor, stage, zonation_coord, zone_probs, entropy] + score-coloured UMAP.

Step 5 — Validate the placement (positive control)

Input: A4 restricted to Paper 1's healthy donors.

In healthy liver zonation is intact, so the method must recover it: hepatocytes should spread along a clear axis; GLUL/CYP2E1 should rise with the coordinate and ASS1/HAL fall (quantify by Spearman correlation); the classifier should be confident (low entropy). If healthy cells do not separate, stop and fix scoring before touching disease data.

Artifact A5: validation figure + correlation/concordance values.

Step 5b — Is the ruler still a ruler? (the validity controls)

Input: A4 across all stages.

Per stage, run the four diagnostics of §5.6: (i) internal coherence — mean pairwise correlation among pericentral genes (and among periportal genes); (ii) cross-program anti-correlation — correlation between the two module scores across cells; (iii) split-half reproducibility — correlate two coordinates from random half-signatures; (iv) program-off vs restriction-lost — track each module's mean magnitude alongside the co-expression rate. Together these say whether and how the ruler degrades, so the Step-6 collapse is interpreted correctly rather than conveniently.

Artifact A5b: per-stage coherence / anti-correlation / split-half / decomposition plots.

Step 6 — Quantify the collapse across stages (headline)

Inputs: A4 (all stages) + A5/A5b (validated).

6a — metrics per stage (report several for robustness): the classifier prediction-entropy (headline); the spread/bimodality of the coordinate distribution; axis separability (silhouette of the two ends, or per-stage portal-vs-central AUC); and the marker anti-correlation from 5b.
6b — trend test. Test the ordered Healthy→end-stage trend (Jonckheere–Terpstra or Spearman on stage rank).
6c — honest error bars. Bootstrap over donors (not cells) for confidence intervals, so the trend is not an artefact of one patient (pseudoreplication guard).
6d — negative control. Shuffle stage labels; the trend must vanish.

Artifact A6: the collapse curve + per-stage distributions + trend p-value (answers H1).

Step 7 — Which genes drive it (zone-resolved DE, FDR-controlled)

Inputs: A4 + A2.

Bin hepatocytes into pseudo-zones (portal / mid / central terciles of the coordinate). Within a zone, test each gene across disease stages — a negative-binomial / Poisson GLM with stage as the predictor (the Exercise 2 likelihood framework, maximally in-scope) or Wilcoxon as the field-standard fallback. Apply Benjamini–Hochberg across all genes; threshold q<0.05; report effect sizes alongside q-values.

Artifact A7: per-zone DE tables (gene, log-FC, p, q) + volcano plots + the shortlist of earliest/strongest collapsing programs (answers H2).

Step 8 — Link de-zonation to plasticity

Inputs: A4 + plasticity markers (KRT7, KRT19, SOX9; + Paper 1 plasticity genes).

Score each hepatocyte for a cholangiocyte/plasticity signature; test whether high-entropy (de-zonated) hepatocytes are enriched for it — correlation across cells, plus a zonated-vs-de-zonated group comparison (Mann–Whitney, FDR-corrected if several signatures).

Artifact A8: figure + statistic linking lost zonal identity to gained plasticity (answers H3).

6.4   Optional bonus (stretch) — does the collapse track inherited MASLD risk? (Paper 3)

If the core results land early, a high-value extension brings in Paper 3 (Nat Genet 2026), which maps MASLD genetic risk variants to their cell-type regulatory elements and target genes (via snATAC-seq + a massively-parallel reporter assay). The question: are the genes that drive our de-zonation collapse (the Step-7 hits) enriched for inherited-risk target genes? If they are, the zonal breakdown is not merely a downstream consequence of disease — it is partly written into the genome, linking a cell-state phenomenon to inherited susceptibility.

How. Take the FDR-significant collapse-driver genes from Step 7 and Paper 3's published risk-variant target-gene list, and test their overlap with a hypergeometric / Fisher test against a matched background (BH-corrected). It is a pure gene-list intersection — light, no new heavy data. A second, lighter stretch: correlate the Step-6 collapse curve with a Paper 4 immune-infiltration trajectory across matched stages.

Why it's a strong bonus, not a dependency It is genuinely additive (genetics × cell-state), cheap (a gene-set enrichment test you already know), and strictly optional — the project stands fully on H1–H3 without it, so it can never sink the timeline.

6.7   Statistical rigor checklist

  • Significance always at BH-FDR q<0.05, never raw p.
  • Stage effect via an ordered trend test, not pairwise only.
  • Donor-level resampling, never 69k cells treated as independent.
  • A positive control (Step 5) and a negative control (shuffled labels).
  • Classifier judged by held-out accuracy on Paper 2 before use on Paper 1.
  • Effect sizes reported beside p/q.
